The Four Brother

The earliest origins of the Materum clan began with four brothers traveling on a small boat called a banca riding up the coast of northern Luzon. It is unrecorded where they came from or where they were going. All we know is that they were sailing along the coastline of Northern Luzon, and they spent little time on land, only to restock their supplies for their voyage.

At one coastal village, the youngest brother tasted the local fruit. It was soft, sweet, and pleasing to both the eye and tongue. "The fruit is bright and sweet. I will remain here," he said. The remaining 3 brothers understood and set sail northward, leaving him behind.

At another rest stop (near modern-day Ilocos Norte), the second brother enjoyed the company of the native villagers. He saw young men who were as strong, courageous, and ambitious as his three brothers. He saw many elders with great wisdom—much greater than his own—who recounted enthralling stories. But most of all, he saw many beautiful smooth-skinned young women. They were gentle and kind, talking with sincerity and grace. And to his surprise, they looked at him with the same eagerness as he saw them. "Surely, I can find a wife in this village and build a family here." He said to himself. "I will remain here," said the second brother. The remaining 2 brothers understood and set sail, travelling east along the northern coastline of Luzon, leaving him behind.

Only two brothers were left. Running low of supplies, they found rest on a beach near a jungle thick with gnarled trees and dense underbrush. "I will remain here," said the third brother. "Why here?" the eldest asked. "The people are small, weak, and uninviting. The land is wild and uncultivated. The trees bear but little fruit.

"What can be fun without challenge? I will till the land to bear fruit. I will build a small and successful barangay using wise words and calloused hands. I will make a name for myself in this land of opportunity. I will remain here."

The last and eldest brother understood, climbed over the gunwale of the boat onto the deck, rigged the ship, and set sail. We don't know where the eldest brother went, whether he reached his intended destination or took residence in some favorable land. Perhaps he travelled northward to Taiwan, or southward to Mindanao. Perhaps he was lost at sea—though that is doubtful because the four brothers were excellent sailors. Whatever that case, neither he nor his descendants were ever heard from again. Whether or not he found a suitable land of promise is pure conjecture.

We are descendants of the third brother.

Florintino Materum

The early Materums were a peculiarity. Unlike the native indigenous groups of Northern Luzon, the early Materums were tall and lanky—towering over 6 feet tall.

Florintino Materum, a descendent of the third son was one such man, standing 6 foot, 3 inches with dark ebony skin and high cheekbones.

Florintino was known for his mischievous wit.

Saving the Aetan man

One day while travelling from one barangay to another, he heard shouts for help in the distance.

"Perhaps it is a trap," he thought to himself. He decided to ignore the please for help, but as he continued his planned route, am by myself so I better ignore it, the sound grew louder. He could hear the sound of rushing water from the Abulog river. "Well, since this plea is on the way to my destination, I will look."

Walking carefully to not make much sound, he followed the cries for help until he found a small child desperately holding onto a branch in the middle of rushing water. The child was too small to wade the water without being swept away by the strong current. Had the river been anymore violent, he would have walked away. For he had no interest in risking his own life to save a stranger, even a child. But Florintino that he could wade the water without much difficulty and save the child.

Florintino laid down his knapsack, waded the water, picked up the child with ease, and carried him over to dry land.

Looking at the child, he realized that it wasn't a child at all but rather a full grown man, an Aeta! The Aetas were an indigenous nomadic hunter-gatherers that lived high in the bundoks. They were characteristically short—all were less than 5 feet tall, had onyx black skin, and kinked hair. The Aetas kept to themselves; only descending the mountains on rare occasions to sell goods like trinkets, weaved baskets, and wild fruits. The word for boondocks in English meaning "an isolated, remote countryside" comes from the word bundok, a Tagalog word meaning mountain. This is because most people—other than the Aetas—preferred to settle closer to sea-level: in the gentle sloping foothills, in the river valleys, and near coastlines.

Florintino patted on the mans back until the Aetan man expelled a mouth full of water in coughing fits. The man, grateful that Florintino saved his life, motioned him up the mountain to spend time with his family, and Florintino stayed for a few months with his tribe.

Florintino was adept at learning languages, already knowing Ilocano, Visayan, and Tagalog, so learning the tribe's language was quick work. He stayed for a few months. The Aetan men appreciated his help in picking high fruits and lifting heavy objects. Florintino also liked the Aetan people too for they reminded him of his own nomadic lifestyle.

From then on, Florintino would visit small pockets of nomadic tribes in between his travels from barangay to barangay. Each Aetan tribe would be wary at first but soon welcomed him when they realized he spoke their language with fluency and because he brought gifts such as food, rope, and knives. But most of all, they enjoyed his stories, especially stories of other Aetan people living in distant mountains. These gifts were mere trifles to Florintino but invaluable treasures to the Aetan people.

A Reflection

When I was younger, I would hear about Florintino's feats of strength. He could single-handedly lift up a boat. He could uproot bamboo trees. He could wrangle crocodiles. But when I pressed them for more detail, I found that these "boats" were a type of light canoe weighing usually carried by two people. The bamboo trees were most likely not fully mature. Anyone able to overhead press 100 lbs and deadlift 200lbs could easily accomplish these feats of strength. It was until I began researching for this book that I discovered that the crocodile in question was a bukarot, a smaller species of crocodile that in full maturity would span to only 4 feet long.

Still, his feats of strength were impressive. But I think it reveals more about his cleverness than about his herculean strength. He was born more than a head taller and his size difference naturally made him slightly stronger than his peers. He used that slight advantage to make a name for himself.

Also, everytime family members would talk about Florintino, they woukd prelude the story by saying, "This was before the Materums and Amayuns became Christian." Florintino was not celebrated for his virtue. His most notorious stories were self-serving. Other times, he was a conman. Of course, there is more to him that the stories told to me. There were no stories of infidelity, violence, or domestic abuse. Yet, the legacy that he left behind and his most memorable qualities, are exemplified in the stories shared above. It makes you wonder what stories loved ones will say about you when you die.
